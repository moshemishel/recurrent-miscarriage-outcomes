## Abstract

**Objective:** This study aimed to evaluate perinatal outcomes in women with a history of recurrent pregnancy loss.

**Study Design:** Retrospective cohort study of singleton and nonanomalous gestations at ≥ 20 weeks who delivered at an academic institution. The exposed group was defined as women with a history of ≥ 2 consecutive spontaneous abortions (SABs) at < 12 weeks. These women were compared with women with a history of ≤ 1 SAB at < 12 weeks. The primary outcome was preterm birth (PTB) at < 37 weeks. Secondary outcomes included gestational age at delivery, gestational diabetes, small for gestational age birth weight, hypertensive diseases of pregnancy, fetal demise, cesarean delivery, and a composite of neonatal complications (5-minute Apgar score < 5, perinatal death, and NICU admission). Multivariable logistic regression was performed to adjust for confounders.

**Results:** Of 17,670 women included, 235 (1.3%) had a history of ≥ 2 consecutive SABs. Compared with women with a history of ≤ 1 SAB, women with ≥ 2 consecutive SABs were not more likely to have a PTB (19.6 vs. 14.0%, p = 0.01, adjusted odds ratios (AOR): 0.91, 95% confidence interval [CI]: 0.62–1.33). However, they were more likely to deliver at an earlier mean gestational age (37.8 ± 3.4 vs. 38.6 ± 2.9 weeks, p < 0.01) and to have gestational diabetes (12.3 vs. 6.6%, p < 0.01, AOR: 1.69, 95% CI: 1.10–2.59). Other outcomes were similar between the two groups.

**Conclusion:** A history of ≥ 2 consecutive SABs was not associated with an increased incidence of PTB but may be associated with gestational diabetes in a subsequent pregnancy.

## Introduction

Although spontaneous abortion (SAB) is common, affecting approximately 20% of all clinically recognized pregnancies, recurrent SAB (i.e., recurrent pregnancy loss [RPL]) is far less common. It is estimated that only 2 to 3% of women will experience two consecutive SABs, and < 1% of women will experience three or more consecutive SABs. Although some have defined RPL as three or more consecutive pregnancy losses, it has been previously shown that the risk for repeat miscarriage is similar in women who have had two compared with three prior SABs. Further, women with three or more losses are not more likely to have a determining cause found after evaluation than are women with two consecutive losses. Therefore, the American Society for Reproductive Medicine (ASRM) recommends that an evaluation is undertaken in women who have two or more consecutive pregnancy losses.

Not surprisingly, women with a history of recurrent SABs often have significant anxiety in a subsequent pregnancy. Although the incidence of subsequent miscarriage has been well defined in women with a history of recurrent miscarriage, previous studies that have evaluated perinatal outcomes in subsequent pregnancies have shown mixed results with some, but not all studies showing increased rates of preterm birth (PTB) and other adverse perinatal outcomes, including gestational diabetes, hypertensive disorders, mode of delivery, small for gestational age birth weight, intrauterine fetal demise, and perinatal loss. The ability to adequately counsel these patients regarding their future risks is of utmost importance for both patient counseling and management.

Given the inconsistency in results from prior studies, the aim of this study was to evaluate perinatal outcomes in women with a history of recurrent consecutive SAB in a large population at a single academic center. Our goal was to further define additional risks so that it can be determined what if any additional intervention is indicated in subsequent pregnancies.

---

## Materials and Methods

We performed a retrospective cohort study of women who delivered at a single academic institution (University of Alabama at Birmingham Hospital) between January 1, 2008 and December 31, 2017. Institutional review board approval was obtained. Eligible women were identified through our perinatal database, which contains searchable medical records.

Women were considered eligible for inclusion if they had a singleton pregnancy at 20 weeks' gestational age. We elected to include only those beyond 20 weeks as the risk of recurrent miscarriage is well studied; our intent was to evaluate outcomes in women with continuing pregnancies. Additionally, only women who reached 20 weeks' gestation were at risk for the outcomes of interest. Women were excluded if they received prenatal care in our institution but delivered elsewhere or if they carried a pregnancy with a known fetal anomaly. Markers of aneuploidy, such as increased nuchal thickness, echogenic intracardiac focus, and hypoplastic nasal bone, were not considered structural anomalies.

Two exposure groups were identified. The exposed group was defined as women who had a history of ≥ 2 consecutive SABs that occurred at < 12 weeks' gestational age. These women were compared with women with a history of ≤ 1 SAB at < 12 weeks' gestation. We chose to restrict our analysis to only those with a history of SAB < 12 weeks, as women with losses in the mid-trimester may have different etiologies of their loss (e.g., cervical insufficiency or preterm labor). If we were unable to verify both the gestational age of the pregnancy loss and that the losses occurred consecutively, these women would have been excluded from the analysis. For those women who had more than one delivery at our institution following their history of recurrent SAB, only the earliest subsequent pregnancy that reached 20 weeks was included.

### Primary Outcome

Preterm birth < 37 weeks.

### Secondary Outcomes

- Gestational age at delivery
- Gestational diabetes
- Small for gestational age birth weight
- Hypertensive diseases of pregnancy
- Fetal demise
- Cesarean delivery
- Composite of adverse neonatal outcomes

### Outcome Definitions

The neonatal composite outcome consisted of the following individual outcomes: 5-minute Apgar score < 5, perinatal death, and admission to the neonatal intensive care unit.

Gestational diabetes was diagnosed by Carpenter–Coustan criteria based on screening and diagnostic testing performed at 24 to 28 weeks.

Small for gestational age was defined as birth weight < 10th percentile for gestational age as calculated by the Brenner's nomogram.

Hypertensive diseases of pregnancy included gestational hypertension and preeclampsia, as recorded by the delivery physician.

Perinatal death was defined as the sum of infant deaths that occur at less than 28 days of age and fetal deaths with a gestational age of 20 weeks or more. Although fetal demise is included within the outcome of perinatal death, we thought it was important to look at fetal demise as a separate outcome, as this has the potential to influence obstetric management of these women during future pregnancies.

### Statistical Analysis

Comparisons were made using the Student's t-test, Chi-squared test, or Fisher's exact test as appropriate. Statistical significance was defined by p < 0.05. Multivariable logistic regression was used to adjust for potential confounders. Confounders considered in the models included maternal age, race, parity, body mass index (BMI), payer status, hypertension, pregestational diabetes, and prior PTB. All statistical analyses were performed using SAS, version 9.4 (Cary, NC).

## Results

During the selected timeframe, there were 29,356 deliveries of nonanomalous, singleton pregnancies at ≥ 20 weeks at the institution. These deliveries occurred in 22,447 women and 17,670 (78.7%) of whom met the inclusion criteria (Figure 1). Of the eligible women, 235 (1.3%) had a history of ≥ 2 consecutive SABs. These women were compared with the remaining 17,435 women with ≤ 1 prior SAB.

The primary outcome of PTB < 37 weeks occurred in 19.6% of those with ≥ 2 prior SABs and in 14.0% of women with ≤ 1 prior SAB (p = 0.01). After adjusting for confounding factors, this difference was no longer significant, with an AOR of 0.91 (95% CI: 0.62–1.33). However, women with a history of ≥ 2 SABs were more likely to deliver at an earlier mean gestational age, delivering at 37.8 ± 3.4 weeks compared with 38.6 ± 2.9 weeks (p < 0.01) in women with ≤ 1 prior SAB.

Gestational diabetes occurred more frequently in women with a history of ≥ 2 SABs, occurring in 12.3% compared with 6.6% of those with ≤ 1 prior SAB (p < 0.01). This remained significant after adjusting for confounders, with an adjusted odds ratio of 1.69 (95% CI: 1.10–2.59). The remaining secondary outcomes, including small for gestational age birth weight, hypertensive diseases of pregnancy, fetal demise, cesarean delivery, and the neonatal composite outcome, were similar between the two groups.

## Discussion

In this retrospective cohort, the study found that women with a history of two or more consecutive SABs did not have an increased risk of PTB < 37 weeks in a subsequent pregnancy. However, when compared with women with a history of zero or one previous SAB, women with a history of ≥ 2 consecutive abortions did have an increased risk of having a subsequent pregnancy affected by gestational diabetes. The risks of other maternal and neonatal complications were similar between the two groups.

Results of prior studies are inconsistent regarding the link between RPL and adverse perinatal outcomes. Reginald et al (1987) showed increased rates of preterm delivery, small for gestational age, and perinatal mortality. Jivraj et al (2001) also found increased rates of PTB, small for gestational age neonates, and perinatal loss, as well as an increased rate of cesarean delivery. Hughes et al showed increased rates of gestational diabetes and chronic hypertension in those with a history of multiple pregnancy losses. However, PTB and small for gestational age were not significantly different. Field and Murphy found an increased risk of PTB, perinatal mortality, and admission to the neonatal intensive care unit in women with a history of RPL. Dempsey et al performed one of the few prospective studies to evaluate this question. They examined a small group of patients (n = 42) with a history of RPL and found that subsequent pregnancy outcomes were similar between the two groups. However, it is noteworthy that Dempsey et al included only those women with a history of unexplained pregnancy losses.

An important difference between the present study and the above studies is that all of the above included women who had three or more prior pregnancy losses. The authors are aware of few other studies that have examined perinatal outcomes in women with a history of RPL as defined by two or more consecutive losses. A study by Shapira et al is one of these few studies. In this analysis, they compared women who had primary RPL with women who had secondary RPL, finding that women with primary RPL had a higher incidence of adverse perinatal outcomes, including PTB, gestational diabetes, and fetal growth abnormalities (both small for gestational age and large for gestational age).

Due to the nature of RPL, studies of its impact on pregnancy outcomes are necessarily cohorts or case controls, with their inherent limitations of measured and unmeasured confounding. Indeed, women with RPL have risk factors for adverse pregnancy outcomes including older age and high-risk comorbidities. This finding in the study as presented is also seen in other published studies. As RPL is associated with older maternal age and chronic medical conditions, confounding variables will always be an issue and will bias toward finding more unfavorable outcomes in women with RPL. However, the large sample size and detailed data available regarding these comorbidities enabled the researchers to adjust for these confounding variables, and after adjusting, an apparent increase in the risk of PTB in women with RPL was no longer present.

In addition to the above description of confounding, several other limitations are acknowledged:

1. RPL was defined based on patient self-report, resulting in possible misclassification errors and biasing the results toward the null.

2. The number of women in the cohort of ≥ 2 prior SABs was only 1.3%, which is lower than is typically reported for two or more consecutive pregnancy losses. The authors feel that this is most likely related to the fact that they excluded women in whom the gestational age and timing of succession of SABs was not clearly documented.

3. The authors do not know if women in the cohort had an underlying cause of their RPLs and are unable to determine whether the findings differ based on etiology. Although this has the potential to introduce bias into the results, it is important to note that at least 50% of women who undergo an evaluation for RPL will not have an etiology found.

4. Multiple comparisons were made, which raises the possibility that the findings in regards to gestational diabetes were due to chance alone.

---

## Conclusion

The study findings indicate that women with a history of ≥ 2 consecutive SABs who have a subsequent pregnancy that reaches at least 20 weeks' gestation do not have an increased risk of preterm delivery < 37 weeks. However, these women may have an increased incidence of gestational diabetes in a subsequent pregnancy. Future studies evaluating this increased risk of gestational diabetes may be warranted. Although this information is useful for counseling women with a history of RPL on their future pregnancy outcomes, it is important to note that other comorbid conditions (e.g., maternal diabetes and obesity) have the potential to influence these outcomes, and thus, counseling should be individualized, taking into account these important comorbidities.
