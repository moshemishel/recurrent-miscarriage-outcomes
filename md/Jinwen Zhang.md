**Objective:** To examine the associations between a history of recurrent miscarriage (RM) and adverse obstetric and perinatal outcomes in the subsequent pregnancy that progressed beyond 24 weeks.
**Design:** Retrospective cohort study.
**Setting:** A large tertiary maternity hospital.
**Patient(s):** All women who booked for antenatal care and delivery between January 2014 and August 2021 were recorded. The study was limited to women with a singleton pregnancy, and to avoid intraperson correlation, we selected the first record of delivery from each mother in the study, leaving 108,792 deliveries for analysis. Obstetric and perinatal outcomes were compared among 1994 women (1.83%) with a history of ≥2 miscarriages (RM), 11,477 women (10.55%) with a history of 1 miscarriage, and 95,321 women (87.62%) with no history of miscarriage, respectively.
**Intervention(s):** Women with a history of ≥2 miscarriages or RM.
**Main Outcome Measure(s):** Obstetric complications included gestational diabetes mellitus, preeclampsia (subclassified as preterm and term preeclampsia), placenta previa, placenta accreta, and fetal distress. Perinatal outcomes included emergency cesarean section, elective cesarean section, induction, postpartum hemorrhage, preterm birth, stillbirth, Apgar score <7 at 5 minutes, neonatal asphyxia, neonatal sex, congenital; malformation, low birth weight, and neonatal death.
**Result(s):** After adjusting for relevant confounders, there was an increased risk of adverse obstetric and perinatal outcomes in a subsequent pregnancy for women with a history of RM, specifically for placental dysfunction disorders: preterm preeclampsia (risk ratio [RR] = 1.58; 95% confidence interval [CI], 1.03–2.32), preterm birth (RR = 1.34; 95% CI, 1.15–1.54)], and abnormal placentation, that is placenta previa (RR = 1.78; 95% CI, 1.36–2.28), and placenta accreta (RR = 4.19; 95% CI, 2.75–6.13).
**Conclusion(s):** Significant associations existed between a history of RM and the occurrence of adverse obstetric and perinatal outcomes including placental dysfunction disorders and abnormal placentation. These findings may contribute to the early detection and appropriate intervention for placenta-associated diseases in women with a history of RM, with the goal of avoiding or reducing the associated detrimental effects. ## INTRODUCTION

Miscarriage generally is defined as the loss of an intrauterine pregnancy before viability (1). The reported incidence of miscarriage is up to 15% for all clinical pregnancies (2). Recurrent miscarriage (RM) is defined as the loss of ≥2 pregnancies before the 24th week of gestation without the stipulation that these losses must be consecutive (1, 3). Recurrent miscarriage is estimated to occur in approximately 1%–3% of women trying to conceive (1, 2, 4, 5). Most research in this patient group has addressed risk factors associated with RM and the risk of a subsequent pregnancy resulting in a miscarriage (6), but the impact on other pregnancy outcomes remains to be addressed. Whether this population constitutes a high-risk obstetric population during the latter stages of pregnancy remains unclear. There is a dearth of information and inconsistent results on obstetrical and perinatal outcomes among women who achieve potential viability in a subsequent pregnancy (7–9). To date, most studies are small, fail to adjust for potential confounders (10, 11), or fail to select appropriate controls (12). Thus, there is a need for large, well-controlled studies that adjust for known relevant confounders for adverse obstetric and perinatal outcomes, including age, prepregnancy body mass index (BMI), parity, education level, smoking and alcohol use, assisted reproductive techniques, and medical history (13).

The aim of this study was to determine if a history of RM is a risk factor for adverse obstetric and perinatal outcomes among pregnancies that progressed beyond 24 weeks. The study was conducted in a large, contemporary cohort of women booked for antenatal care and delivery in a large tertiary maternity hospital setting in Shanghai, China. We hypothesized that a history of RM is associated with an increased risk of adverse obstetric and perinatal outcomes, especially diseases involving the placenta.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

### Study Population

This retrospective cohort study was conducted using electronic antenatal and delivery records of all pregnant women who attended the International Peace Maternity and Child Health Hospital, between January 2014 and August 2021 (Supplemental Fig. S1, available online). This hospital is a large, urban maternity hospital that delivers care to approximately 16,000 women annually. There was a total of 118,375 deliveries in the database. A total of 5.5% of women contributed >1 delivery to the database. To avoid intraperson correlation, we selected the first record of delivery for each mother in the study (N = 111,918). The study was limited to women with a singleton pregnancy, leaving 108,792 deliveries for analysis.

Comprehensive records were available routinely for all deliveries of ≥24 weeks' gestation. A viable pregnancy was defined as delivery beyond 24 weeks. The dataset included demographic data, medical history, information on reproductive history and complications during pregnancy, delivery, and the neonatal period. Neonatal records also were available for all children up until hospital discharge. Any woman who was booked for antenatal care but delivered elsewhere was not included in the cohort.

### Exposure Variable and Groups

The main exposure variable was the number of prior spontaneous miscarriages. Study participants were subdivided into 3 groups based on their previous obstetric history. Women with ≥2 previous miscarriages were categorized into the RM group regardless of whether the miscarriages occurred consecutively. Recurrent miscarriage was considered as unexplained when no definite cause could be found after diagnostic workup, excluding conditions leading to RM, such as autoimmune disease (antiphospholipid antibody syndrome (APS), systemic lupus erythematosus, and so forth), prothrombotic and thrombotic conditions, chromosomal anomalies, congenital/acquired uterine anomalies, infection, and endocrine disease (polycystic ovary syndrome, hyperprolactinemia, thyroid dysfunction, diabetes, and so forth) (14–16). Women with a history of a single miscarriage were categorized in the single prior miscarriage group. All other women were included in the comparison group. Only the RM group and comparison group were reported.

### Sociodemographic Characteristics and Medical History

Information on the following sociodemographic characteristics was extracted from the patient information system: maternal age, prepregnancy BMI, marital status, insurance, ethnicity, parity, residence, education, use of assisted reproductive technologies, smoking, and alcohol use. Prepregnancy BMI was recorded and classified as follows: underweight, BMI <18.5 kg/m²; normal weight, BMI = 18.5–23.9 kg/m²; overweight, BMI ≥24–<28 kg/m²; and obesity, BMI ≥28 kg/m² (17). Details about smoking and alcohol use were classified according to the reported use of alcohol or smoking at the first antenatal visit. Assisted reproductive technologies included intrauterine insemination or any type of in vitro fertilization. Medical history that was documented included diabetes, chronic hypertension, heart disease, asthma, anemia, hypo- or hyperthyroidism, viral hepatitis, TORCH infection (cytomegalovirus, toxoplasmosis, herpes, simplex virus, and rubella), sexually transmitted disease, APS, and polycystic ovary syndrome.

### Outcomes of Subsequent Pregnancy

Diagnoses were coded by physicians according to the International Classification of Diseases, 10th revision (18). Obstetric complications included gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM), preeclampsia (subclassified as preterm and term preeclampsia), placental abruption, placenta previa, placenta accreta, and fetal distress. Perinatal outcomes included emergency cesarean section (CS), elective CS, induction, postpartum hemorrhage, preterm birth (<37 weeks), Apgar score <7 at 5 minutes, stillbirth, neonatal asphyxia, neonatal sex, congenital malformation, low birth weight (<2500 g), and neonatal death. Stillbirth was defined as fetal death at ≥28 weeks of gestation or where the birthweight was ≥1000 g, in accordance with the World Health Organization's definition of a third-trimester stillbirth (19). Therefore, analysis of stillbirth was restricted to births at ≥28 weeks (N = 108,745).

Emergency CS occurred in labor or before labor, and deliveries with spontaneous onset or induction of labor were included. Gestational age was estimated based on the first day of the last menstrual period.

### Statistical Analyses

Continuous variables of normal distribution were described using mean and standard deviation, and categorical variables were described as percentages. The χ² test or Fisher's exact test was used to compare differences between groups where appropriate.

To examine the associations between history of RM and obstetric and perinatal outcomes, log binomial regression was used to estimate risk ratios (RRs) and their 95% confidence intervals (CIs). In a few instances, the models did not converge and Poisson regression with a robust error variance was used. (20) Covariates with missing values were assigned to the largest category. Then multivariable log binomial regression models were developed, adjusting for potential confounding factors including sociodemographic characteristics and medical history (which showed a significant difference in Table 1). These factors were chosen based on of their known or potential association with RM and obstetric and perinatal outcomes.

To explore for effect modification by parity, we stratified women into 2 groups: nulliparous and multiparous. Women with RM were divided into 2 groups: primary RM (≥2 miscarriages with no prior viable pregnancy) and secondary RM (≥2 miscarriages and at least 1 previous viable pregnancy). These women were compared with their nulliparous or multiparous counterparts without a history of miscarriage.

To confirm the modification effect of parity, a multiplicative interaction term between RM history and parity was included in the multivariable log binomial regression models. Effect modification was considered statistically significant if the interaction term *P* value was <.05.

Several sensitivity analyses were performed to test the robustness of our results. Sensitivity analyses of patients with unexplained RM versus no spontaneous miscarriage history was conducted. We observed that pregnant women with a single previous miscarriage behaved similarly to pregnant women with no previous miscarriage history (5, 11, 21–23). Thus, we combined those with a history of a single miscarriage and those with no history of miscarriage, adding the analyses comparing the women with RM history to those without. As some covariates (i.e., prepregnancy BMI and education level) with missing values account for 9.7%, the third sensitivity analysis was conducted by a complete case analysis, limiting the study subjects to women with complete prepregnancy BMI and education level information.

All analyses were performed using the SAS for Windows, V.9.4 (SAS Institute, Cary, NC), with 2-tailed tests and a significance level of *P*<.05.

### Ethics Approval

The study was approved by the institutional review boards and the (GKLW) 2017-121 was obtained on August 23, 2018. Individual patient consent was not deemed necessary as the analysis was based on routinely collected data and was anonymized for analysis. This study followed the Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology (STROBE) reporting guidelines for retrospective cohort studies.

## RESULTS

A total of 1994 women (1.83%) had a history of ≥2 miscarriages and 11,477 women (10.55%) had a history of only 1 miscarriage, while the remaining 95,321 (87.62%) had no history of miscarriage. Of the 1994 women with a history of RM, 1351 (67.75%) had primary RM and 643 (32.25%) had secondary RM.

The sociodemographic characteristics and medical history of the women were summarized in Table 1. Compared with the women with no miscarriage history, women with a history of RM were more likely to be older (33.49 ± 4.17 vs. 30.65 ± 3.91; *P*<.001), overweight or obese (*P*<.001), single status (1.55% vs. 0.76%; *P*<.001), have a lower educational level (*P*<.001), and to have had assisted conception (13.74% vs. 5.58%; *P*<.0001). Those with RM were less likely to have health insurance (72.12% vs. 76.01%; *P*<.001), be nulliparous (67.75% vs. 77.52%; *P*<.0001), to reside locally (63.44% vs. 69.15%; *P*<.001), and were more likely to have pregestational diabetes (0.75% vs. 0.32%; *P* = 0.0008), chronic hypertension (0.90% vs. 0.31%; *P*<.0001), hypothyroidism (8.32% vs. 4.68%; *P*<.0001), and APS (2.61% vs. 0.05%; *P*<.0001).

The descriptive analyses and univariable and multivariable log binomial analyses of obstetric and perinatal outcomes of women according to the history of miscarriage are summarized in Table 2. Compared with the women with no history of spontaneous miscarriage, women with a history of RM were more likely to have preterm preeclampsia (1.25% vs. 0.55%; adjusted RR [aRR] = 1.58; 95% CI, 1.03–2.32), placenta previa (3.06% vs. 1.19%; aRR = 1.78; 95% CI, 1.36–2.28), placenta accreta (1.45% vs. 0.20%; aRR = 4.19; 95% CI, 2.75–6.13), preterm birth (8.68% vs. 5.48%; aRR = 1.34; 95% CI, 1.15–1.54), and a slight increase in GDM (19.96% vs. 13.07%; aRR = 1.16; 95% CI, 1.06–1.16) and elective CS (48.75% vs. 34.89%; aRR = 1.15; 95% CI, 1.07–1.22).

The modifying effect of parity on the results were explored as shown in Table 3 and Supplemental Table S1 and S2 (available online). Compared with the nulliparous women with no history of spontaneous miscarriage, women with a history of RM in the nulliparous group had a significantly increased risk of placenta previa (aRR = 1.96; 95% CI, 1.41–2.66), elective CS (aRR = 1.22; 95% CI, 1.13–1.32), and a lower risk of fetal distress (aRR = 0.87; 95% CI, 0.77–0.99). These findings were not observed in the multiparous group. These women in the multiparous group had a significantly increased risk of preeclampsia (aRR = 1.58; 95% CI, 1.20–2.05), preterm preeclampsia (aRR = 1.71; 95% CI, 1.38–2.11), emergency CS (aRR = 1.31; 95% CI, 1.02–1.65), and postpartum hemorrhage (aRR = 1.89; 95% CI, 1.10–3.02). These associations were not observed in the nulliparous group. However, nulliparous women with a history of RM had a significantly lower risk of induction (aRR = 0.87; 95% CI, 0.78-0.96), whereas multiparas with RM had significantly higher risk of induction (aRR = 1.22; 95% CI, 1.01–1.45) than their controls without a history of miscarriage. As shown in Supplementary Table S2, testing for the effect modification of parity was positive for the outcomes of preeclampsia (overall; *P* for interaction = .018), fetal distress (*P* for interaction = .0006), emergency CS (*P* for interaction = .001), elective CS (*P* for interaction = .0002), labor induction (*P* for interaction <.0001), and postpartum hemorrhage (*P* for interaction = .047).

Regarding the sensitivity analyses, the results of sensitivity analysis of patients with unexplained RM versus no spontaneous miscarriage history also showed similar results, especially with respect to the placenta-associated disorders (Table 4). Among 1994 RM women, 46% (921/1994) were categorized as having unexplained RM. Among these women, remained significantly increased risk of preterm preeclampsia (aRR = 1.97, 95% CI 1.08–3.28) and placenta accreta (aRR = 3.92, 95% CI 2.06–6.75), and preterm birth (aRR = 1.52, 95% CI 1.23–1.85) relative to controls without miscarriage. In another sensitivity analyses, we combined those with a history of a single miscarriage to those with no history of miscarriage as the reference group to compare the women with and without a RM history, and results were very similar to the results above (Supplemental Table S3, available online), as well as the complete case analyses (Supplemental Table S4, available online). All above indicated the robustness of our results.

The obstetric and perinatal outcomes for women with primary RM were compared with those for women with secondary RM (Supplemental Table S5, available online). The adverse outcomes were very similar for primary and secondary RM, except that primary RM groups were more likely to have a diagnosis of fetal distress (15.99% vs. 7.78%, *P*<.0001), a well as to have undergone an induction of labor (21.91% vs. 15.86%, *P* = .0016). Secondary RM groups were prone to have GDM (18.65% vs. 22.71%, *P* = .034).

## DISCUSSION

This study is novel in that we attempt to explore the effects of a history of RM on pregnancy and neonatal outcomes beyond 24 weeks. The prevalence of RM within the cohort was 1.83%. This study reports an increased risk of adverse obstetric and perinatal outcomes in a subsequent pregnancy for women with a history of RM. Adverse outcomes include placental dysfunction disorders (preterm preeclampsia, and preterm birth), and abnormal placentation (placenta previa and placenta accreta). These associations persisted even after adjusting for potential confounders.

Our findings of associations between a history of RM and placental dysfunction disorders support the findings of a previous study (10) that showed that miscarriage and placental dysfunction disorders have a partially common pathogenesis of early placentation failure (24). It has been hypothesized that a complete failure of early placentation may result in miscarriage (25). However, if the failure is partial, the pregnancy might remain viable but with insufficient uterine spiral artery remodeling and a continued imbalance in angiogenic activity during the remaining process (26).

Several sensitivity analyses were performed to test the robustness of our results, revealing the results were similar. The sensitivity analysis of patients with unexplained RM vs. no RM would account more reliably for some of these confounders and give a more accurate assessment of the association between RM and adverse outcomes. Consistent with our results, a previous study confirmed that regardless of the cause of RM, this group represents a high-risk obstetric population for placental dysfunction disorders including preterm labor and small for gestational age (12). Enhanced prenatal assessment and intervention may have the potential to prevent or delay the onset of placental dysfunction disorders in women with a history of RM. Whether these women should be regarded as a high-risk population of preeclampsia or preterm birth which could be benefit from low-dose aspirin prophylactic treatment (27, 28) deserves further investigation.

Other studies failed to demonstrate an association between history of RM and the occurrence of preeclampsia (21, 29, 30). However, previous studies failed to distinguish between early-onset and late-onset preeclampsia. Early-onset or preterm preeclampsia is considered a fetal disorder that is associated typically with placental dysfunction, whereas, late-onset or term preeclampsia is considered a result of an underlying maternal constitutional disorder (31). Here, we distinguished preterm preeclampsia from term preeclampsia, and found that women with a history of RM were likely to have preterm preeclampsia in the next pregnancy. As well, parity may act as an effect modifier as the association was significant only in multiparous women. Multiparas with a history of RM maybe more likely to have impaired placenta function than multiparous women with no history of spontaneous miscarriage.

Because all these aforementioned complications have been linked in previous studies to trophoblastic abnormalities and decidual invasion (12, 32–35), it was hypothesized that women with RM may have underlying abnormal placentation (36). In our study, women with a history of RM had an increased risk of placenta previa and placenta accreta compared with women in the control group, even when taking their CS history into account. This is consistent with previous studies (5, 37). The risk of placenta previa and placenta accreta increases stepwise with each previous miscarriage. Women with a history of RM were more likely to have placenta previa (aRR = 1.78) and placenta accreta (aRR = 4.19) in the subsequent pregnancy regardless of parity. Injury to the uterine wall or endometrium also can cause abnormal placentation in subsequent pregnancies, resulting in increased risk of placenta praevia and placenta accreta. Therefore, the association between a history of miscarriage and these adverse obstetric outcomes could be driven partly by a common cause, perhaps originating in suboptimal endometrial repair and decidualization (1).

This study revealed no risk of congenital malformation or aneuploidy related to RM. However, there has been controversy regarding whether RM increased the risk of a congenital malformation or aneuploidy. Two previous studies conducted in the last century reported that previous miscarriage or RM was associated with congenital malformation (21, 38). Our study and a recent study (30) found no association between RM and congenital malformation or aneuploidy. The absence of such an association may be attributable to the context of early prenatal care combined with a systematic approach to birth defect screening, and noninvasive prenatal testing or preimplantation genetic test of aneuploidy. As women who had pregnancy terminations before 24 weeks were not included in the analysis, we could not rule out an association between a history of RM and early pregnancy termination due to fetal malformation.

Because the prevalence of some pregnancy complications can vary with parity, Jivraj et al. (9) suggested that the control population ideally should be matched on parity with the exposed cohort. The bias would be minimized when comparing the miscarriage group to their selective nulliparous/multiparous control. In addition, our stratified analyses showed parity to act as an effect modifier for several of the associations, also confirmed by the multivariable log binomial regression models. Multiparity amplified the risk of a history of RM. Generally, some obstetric and perinatal outcomes are better in multiparous than in nulliparous women (39). However, our results are consistent with those of a previous study (12) showing that adverse outcomes were very similar for primary RM, that is nulliparous, and secondary RM, that is multiparous. Therefore, the protective effect of a previous healthy pregnancy seems to disappear in multiparous women with RM. Further studies are needed to confirm the results and explore the underlying mechanism.

Our study has limitations. Firstly, the tertiary center setting could have influenced the prevalence of some outcomes. In addition, we only focused on pregnancies that progressed beyond 24 weeks' gestation and as such we cannot generalize our findings to all pregnant women. We did not include pregnancies that ended before 24 weeks in the design of the present study, as the overarching goal of our study was to stratify and report as much information as possible on the obstetric and perinatal outcomes of the investigated subjects. This may be a caveat but not part of the current study design. Thirdly, regarding the exposure variable, we did not distinguish the second trimester miscarriages from the first trimester miscarriages. It has been reported that nearly 80% of miscarriages appear in the first trimester (40). Miscarriages occurring in the second trimester may be of a different etiology (41). Whether the early versus last of miscarriage impacts differentially on subsequent pregnancies needs further investigation. Furthermore, exposure was measured as self-reported miscarriages recorded by doctors, which made it possible to include miscarriages experienced by the women regardless of a need for specialist care (10), and recall bias also solely on doctor may exist. Similarly, a potential limitation may exist regarding some variables being documented based on physician reporting (e.g., APS diagnosis was not made by clinical and laboratorial criteria but rather by doctor or patient self-report). It is possible that there was a more detailed documentation of potential confounders among patients with a history of RM.

In conclusion, we found that women with a history of RM are at increased risk for several adverse outcomes, including placental dysfunction disorder (preterm preeclampsia and preterm birth) and abnormal placentation (placenta previa and placenta accrete), whether or not the miscarriages are consecutive. These results may be useful in guiding the clinical management of pregnancies, especially in the early detection of placenta-associated diseases in women with a history of RM. Further studies are required to confirm these observations.

## Resumen en Español

**Resultados obstétricos y perinatales adversos en pacientes con antecedentes de abortos recurrentes: un estudio de cohorte retrospectivo**

**Objetivo:** Examinar las asociaciones entre antecedentes de abortos recurrentes (AR) y los resultados obstétricos y perinatales adversos en el embarazo subsecuente que progresó más de 24 semanas.

**Diseño:** Estudio de cohorte retrospectivo.

**Entorno:** Un gran hospital terciario de maternidad.

**Paciente(s):** Todas las mujeres que se registraron para recibir atención prenatal y dar a luz entre enero de 2014 y agosto de 2021 fueron registradas. El estudio se limitó a mujeres con un embarazo único, y para evitar la correlación intrapersonal, seleccionamos el primer registro de parto de cada madre en el estudio, lo que dejó 108,792 partos para el análisis. Los resultados obstétricos y perinatales se compararon entre 1994 mujeres (1.83%) con antecedentes de ≥2 abortos (AR), 11,477 mujeres (10.55%) con antecedentes de 1 aborto y 95,321 mujeres (87.62%) sin antecedentes de aborto, respectivamente.

**Intervención(es):** Mujeres con antecedentes de ≥2 abortos o AR

**Medida(s) principal(es) de resultado(s):** Las complicaciones obstétricas incluyeron diabetes mellitus gestacional, preeclampsia (subclasificada como preeclampsia prematura y a término), placenta previa, placenta acreta y sufrimiento fetal. Los resultados perinatales incluyeron cesárea de emergencia, cesárea electiva, inducción del parto, hemorragia posparto, parto prematuro, muerte fetal, puntuación de Apgar <7 a los 5 minutos, asfixia neonatal, sexo neonatal, malformación congénita, bajo peso al nacer y muerte neonatal.

**Resultado(s):** Después de ajustar por factores de confusión relevantes, hubo un mayor riesgo de resultados obstétricos y perinatales adversos en un embarazo posterior para mujeres con antecedentes de abortos recurrentes (AR), específicamente para trastornos de disfunción placentaria: preeclampsia prematura (razón de riesgo [RR] = 1.58; intervalo de confianza [IC] del 95%, 1.03-2.32), parto prematuro (RR = 1.34; IC del 95%, 1.15-1.54), y alteración de la implantación placentaria, es decir, placenta previa (RR = 1.78; IC del 95%, 1.36-2.28), y placenta acreta (RR = 4.19; IC del 95%, 2.75-6.13).

**Conclusión(es):** Se encontraron asociaciones significativas entre antecedentes de abortos recurrentes (AR) y la aparición de resultados obstétricos y perinatales adversos, incluyendo trastornos de disfunción placentaria y anomalías en la implantación placentaria. Estos hallazgos pueden contribuir a la detección temprana e intervención adecuada para enfermedades asociadas a la placenta en mujeres con antecedentes de AR, con el objetivo de evitar o reducir los efectos perjudiciales asociados.
