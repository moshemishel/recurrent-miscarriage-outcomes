## Abstract

*Objective:* The purpose of the present study was to examine the association between spontaneous consecutive recurrent abortions and pregnancy complications such as hypertensive disorders, abruptio placenta, intrauterine growth restriction and cesarean section (CS) in the subsequent pregnancy.

*Methods:* A population-based study comparing all singleton pregnancies in women with and without two or more consecutive recurrent abortions was conducted. Deliveries occurred during the years 1988–2002. Stratified analysis, using a multiple logistic regression model was performed to control for confounders.

*Results:* During the study period 154,294 singleton deliveries occurred, with 4.9% in patients with history of recurrent consecutive abortions. Using a multivariate analysis, with backward elimination, the following complications were significantly associated with recurrent abortions—advanced maternal age, cervical incompetence, previous CS, diabetes mellitus, hypertensive disorders, placenta previa and abruptio placenta, mal-presentations and PROM. A higher rate of CS was found among patients with previous spontaneous consecutive recurrent abortions (15.9% versus 10.9%; OR = 1.6; 95% CI, 1.5–1.7; *P* < 0.001). Another multivariate analysis was performed, with CS as the outcome variable, controlling for confounders such as placenta previa, abruptio placenta, diabetes mellitus, hypertensive disorders, previous CS, mal-presentations, fertility treatments and PROM. A history of recurrent abortion was found as an independent risk factor for CS (OR = 1.2; 95% CI, 1.1–1.3; *P* < 0.001). About 58 cases of inherited thrombophilia were found between the years 2000–2002. These cases were significantly more common in the recurrent abortion as compared to the comparison group (1.2% versus 0.1%; OR = 11.1; 95% CI, 6.5–18.9; *P* < 0.001).

*Conclusion:* A significant association exists between consecutive recurrent abortions and pregnancy complications such as placental abruption, hypertensive disorders and CS. This association persists after controlling for variables considered to coexist with recurrent abortions. Careful surveillance is required in pregnancies following recurrent abortions, for early detection of possible complications.

## 1. Introduction

Recurrent abortions, defined as two or more spontaneous consecutive abortions, occur in 0.5–3% of pregnancies [1]. The etiologies of recurrent abortions are heterogeneous, and a definite cause is found in less than half of the cases [1–3]. Possible causes reported include genetic anomalies, anatomical disorders, hormonal abnormalities and thrombophilia [1–5]. Thrombophilia, a hypercoagulability condition due to congenital or acquired disorders, is probably the risk factor for several events previously considered as idiopathic [2–7]. Recently, an association was documented between thrombophilia and pregnancy complications such as preeclampsia [8,9], placental abruption [10], intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR) [11] and recurrent abortions [3,12–17].

The purpose of the present study was to examine the association between recurrent spontaneous abortions and pregnancy complications such as hypertensive disorders, placental abruption, intrauterine growth restriction and cesarean section (CS) in the subsequent pregnancy.

---

## 2. Materials and methods

A population-based study comparing all singleton pregnancies of patients with and without recurrent abortions was conducted. The study group consisted of patients with two or more previous consecutive spontaneous abortions [1]. Deliveries occurred at the Soroka University Medical Center during the years 1988–2002. This is the only hospital in the Negev, the southern part of Israel, serving the whole obstetrical population. Thus, the study presents non-discriminating data.

Data were collected from the perinatal database that consists of information recorded directly after delivery by obstetricians. Skilled secretaries examined the information routinely before entering it into the database. Coding was done after assessing the medical prenatal care records as well as the routine hospital documents. These procedures assured the completeness and accuracy of the database.

The following clinical characteristics were evaluated: maternal age, parity, gestational age at delivery and birth-weight. The following obstetrical complications were examined: fertility treatments (ovulation induction or in-vitro fertilization), previous CS, recurrent abortions, mild or severe preeclampsia [18], chronic hypertension [19], gestational and pre-gestational diabetes mellitus [20], suspected intrauterine growth restriction, premature rupture of membranes (PROM) and placenta previa. The following labor characteristics and neonatal outcomes were assessed: abruptio placenta, meconium-stained amniotic fluid, mal-presentations, CS, Apgar score at 1 and 5 min less than 7, perinatal mortality, post-partum hemorrhage and maternal blood products transfusions.

Data regarding thrombophilia (International Classification of Disease, 9th revision, Clinical Modification code 286.3) was available for the years 2000–2002. A comparison was performed between patients with and without known inherited thrombophilia.

Statistical analysis was performed with the SPSS package (SPSS, Chicago, IL). Statistical significance was ascertained using the chi-square test, or Fisher’s exact test for differences in qualitative variables and the *t*-test for differences in continuous variables. A multivariate logistic regression model, with backward elimination, was constructed in order to identify independent risk factors associated with previous recurrent abortions. Odds ratios (OR) and their 95% confidence interval (CI) were computed. *P* < 0.05 was considered statistically significant. Stratified analysis, using the Mantel–Haenszel technique, was done to assess the association between recurrent abortions and the risk of CS, while controlling for confounding variables.

## 3. Results

During the study period there were 154,294 singleton deliveries, of which 4.9% (*n* = 7503) occurred in women who delivered after consecutive recurrent abortions. Maternal age was significantly higher in women delivered following repeated abortions (Table 1). In addition, these women had higher birth order (gravidity and parity) compared to the comparison group.

Obstetric risk factors are presented in Table 2. There were significantly higher rates of previous CS, fertility treatments, severe preeclampsia, chronic hypertension, diabetes mellitus cervical incompetence and PROM among patients following recurrent abortions versus the rest of the population.

Pregnancy and labor complications are summarized in Table 3. Women following consecutive repeated abortions had significant higher rates of abruptio placenta, placenta previa, mal-presentations, CS, post-partum hemorrhage, and were more likely to receive blood products transfusions as compared to the comparison group.

Using a multivariate analysis, with backward elimination, the following conditions were significantly associated with recurrent abortions (Table 4): cervical incompetence, placenta previa and abruptio placenta, diabetes mellitus, hypertensive disorders, previous CS, mal-presentations, PROM and advanced maternal age.

Another comparison was performed between early (≤12 weeks gestation) and late abortions (>12 weeks). Women with a history of late abortions tended to be older and to deliver earlier than those after early abortions. Higher rates of CS and treatment with packed-cells transfusions were noted among pregnancies after late versus early abortions (Table 5). However, no significant differences regarding Apgar scores or perinatal mortality were found between the groups.

Inherited thrombophilia, 58 cases, were found in our database between the years 2000–2002. These cases were significantly more common in the recurrent abortion as compared to the comparison group (1.2% versus 0.1%; OR = 11; 95% CI, 6.5–18.9; *P* < 0.001). While comparing recurrent abortion cases with and without known thrombophilia between 2000–2002 (Table 6), no significant differences were noted between the groups. However, higher rates of IUGR, CS, low Apgar scores and perinatal mortality were found among the 22 patients with known thrombophilia as compared to the controls, although the differences did not reach statistical significance.

Controlling for possible confounders such as placenta previa, abruptio placenta, diabetes mellitus, hypertensive disorders, previous CS, mal-presentations, PROM and fertility treatments, using the Mantel–Haenszel technique, the association between a history of consecutive recurrent abortion and CS remained significant (Table 7). Moreover, another multivariate analysis was constructed, with CS as the outcome variable, controlling for all these confounding variables. Again, recurrent abortions were found as an independent risk factor for CS (OR = 1.2; 95% CI, 1.1–1.3; *P* < 0.001).

## 4. Discussion

Recurrent abortions are a significant problem, involving about 5% of our population, opposed to a lower incidence of 0.5–3% in the literature [1]. The higher incidence of recurrent abortions may be explained by inclusion of two consecutive abortions in the analysis. Others, who considered recurrence as three or more abortions, defined as low incidence of 1% [21]. Since recurrent consecutive spontaneous abortions are such a common entity, it is important to clarify whether pregnancies following recurrent abortions are at increased risk for complications. This study demonstrates high rates of pregnancy complications such as abruptio placenta, hypertensive disorders and CS even after controlling for possible confounding variables. Likewise, Reginald et al. [22] evaluated pregnancies of women who had three or more miscarriages and found higher rates of small-for-gestational age newborns, preterm deliveries and even perinatal mortality, as compared to the normal obstetric population. The authors concluded that these observations might serve to alert the clinician dealing with these pregnancies.

There are conflicting reports in the literature relating the relationship between inherited thrombophilia and recurrent pregnancy loss. Few studies did not reveal such an association [23,24]. Rai et al. [23] found the frequency of the Factor V Leiden allele to be 3.5% among 904 patients with a history of recurrent early abortions, 3.9% among 207 women with late abortions, and 4.0% among 150 patients who served as the control group. No statistically significant differences were noted between the groups, on the contrary, up to two thirds of women with idiopathic abortions showed at least one thrombophilic defect in a recent prospective cohort study [17]. Indeed, most studies found a significant association between thrombophilia and recurrent pregnancy loss [3,12–17].

Our study initiated on 1988 and included a period when the evidence of thrombophilia did not exist in common practice. Therefore, the majority of patients did not undergo thrombophilia screening. However, while analyzing data regarding the years 2000–2002, inherited thrombophilia was significantly more common among patients with recurrent spontaneous abortions. While comparing cases with known thrombophilia and the other cases of spontaneous abortions, there seems to be a difference regarding adverse pregnancy outcome, but it did not reach statistical significance. Although factors such as IUGR, CS, low Apgar scores and perinatal mortality were found in higher rates in the thrombophilia group, the small number of cases precluded achieving a statistical significance. Thus, the significant higher rates of pregnancy complications following recurrent spontaneous abortions can only speculatively be attributed to thrombophilia. However, it clearly points out that recurrent spontaneous abortion may be an obstetric risk factor for subsequent adverse pregnancy outcome, and that thrombophilia might be the underlining factor accountable for both entities.

Higher rates of deliveries by CS were found in pregnancies following recurrent abortions. The significant association between abortions and CS was maintained after controlling for confounders. It might be caregiver bias, to choose the seemingly simpler, but not safer mode of Cesarean delivery in high-risk patients (i.e. these with a history of infertility or recurrent abortions [25]).

In conclusion, a significant association exists between consecutive recurrent abortions and pregnancy complications such as abruptio placenta, hypertensive disorders and CS. This association is maintained after controlling for variables thought to coexist with recurrent abortions, and is significant even in patients with two or more consecutive abortions. Careful surveillance is required in pregnancies following recurrent abortions, for early detection of complications. Further prospective studies, testing for inherited thrombophilia, should be carried-out in order to define the contribution of thrombophilia to both, recurrent pregnancy losses and subsequent complications.
