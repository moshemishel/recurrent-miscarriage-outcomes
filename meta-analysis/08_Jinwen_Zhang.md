# Zhang, 2023 — Meta-Analysis Extraction

## Group A: Study Characteristics
- **Authors**: Jinwen Zhang, Xiaorui Liu, Lin Rao, Ruixiang Ma, Weibin Wu, Cailian Chen, Yi Lin
- **Year**: 2023 (received August 26, 2022; revised and accepted April 20, 2023; published online April 29, 2023; print volume 120, No. 3, PT. 2, September 2023)
- **Journal**: Fertility and Sterility (Vol. 120, No. 3, PT. 2, pp. 626–634; DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.fertnstert.2023.04.028; publisher: Elsevier Inc.; journal of the American Society for Reproductive Medicine)
- **Country**: China (Shanghai)
- **Data source**: Electronic antenatal and delivery records from The International Peace Maternity and Child Health Hospital, Shanghai Jiao Tong University — a large urban maternity hospital delivering care to approximately 16,000 women annually; comprehensive records for all deliveries ≥24 weeks' gestation; diagnoses coded by physicians according to ICD-10
- **Study design**: Retrospective cohort study
- **Study period**: January 2014 to August 2021

## Group B: Population
- **Total N**: 108,792 deliveries (singleton pregnancies ≥24 weeks; first delivery per mother selected from 111,918 to avoid intraperson correlation from 118,375 total records with 5.5% contributing >1 delivery)
- **N RM group**: 1,994 women (1.83%) with ≥2 prior miscarriages (RM group)
- **N control group**: 95,321 women (87.62%) with no prior miscarriage history
- **Note**: Also a third group of 11,477 women (10.55%) with history of 1 miscarriage — not the primary comparison; only RM vs. no-miscarriage comparison reported here
- **Inclusion criteria**: Singleton pregnancy; delivery ≥24 weeks' gestation (viable pregnancy defined as delivery beyond 24 weeks); first delivery record per mother; booked for antenatal care at the institution. RM: ≥2 previous miscarriages regardless of whether consecutive.
- **Exclusion criteria**: Multiple pregnancies; women with >1 delivery in database (only first delivery selected); women who booked but delivered elsewhere; RM definition: women with known causes of RM excluded from "unexplained RM" sensitivity analysis but all RM women (both explained and unexplained) included in primary analysis
- **Maternal age RM group**: 33.49 ± 4.17 years (mean ± SD)
- **Maternal age control group**: 30.65 ± 3.91 years (mean ± SD); p < 0.001
- **BMI RM group**: Underweight (<18.5 kg/m²): 8.53%; Normal (18.5–24): 63.49%; Overweight (24–28): 14.14%; Obese (≥28): 3.66%; Unknown: 10.18%
- **BMI control group**: Underweight: 13.73%; Normal: 65.20%; Overweight: 9.84%; Obese: 2.09%; Unknown: 9.13%; p < 0.001 (RM more overweight/obese)
- **Parity**: RM group nulliparous 1,351/1,994 (67.75%), multiparous 643/1,994 (32.25%); control group nulliparous 73,893/95,321 (77.52%), multiparous 21,427/95,321 (22.48%); p < 0.001
- **ART/IVF rate**: RM group 274/1,994 (13.74%); control group 5,321/95,321 (5.58%); p < 0.0001
- **Smoking**: Smoking or drinking: RM group 1.25% vs. control group 0.99%; p = 0.24 (not significant)

## Group C: Exposure & Control Definition
- **RM threshold**: ≥2 previous miscarriages (ESHRE definition: loss of ≥2 pregnancies before 24th week of gestation; not required to be consecutive)
- **Type of losses included**: Any spontaneous miscarriage (not restricted to consecutive); first- and second-trimester combined (not distinguished). RM defined as unexplained when no definite cause found after diagnostic workup (excluding autoimmune disease, prothrombotic/thrombotic conditions, chromosomal anomalies, congenital/acquired uterine anomalies, infection, endocrine disease). Primary RM (≥2 miscarriages, no prior viable pregnancy): n=1,351 (67.75%); secondary RM (≥2 miscarriages, ≥1 prior viable pregnancy): n=643 (32.25%).
- **Gestational age cutoff for "miscarriage"**: Before 24 weeks of gestation (per ESHRE definition used by authors)
- **Dose-response analysis**: No formal dose-response within the RM group. Stratified analyses by parity (nulliparous vs. multiparous) and RM type (explained vs. unexplained, primary vs. secondary). Sensitivity analysis comparing single miscarriage + no miscarriage combined as reference vs. RM showed similar results.
- **Control group definition**: All other women with no history of spontaneous miscarriage who delivered at the institution; 95,321 women (87.62% of total). A separate sensitivity analysis also used "single miscarriage + no miscarriage" as combined reference (n=106,798).

## Group D: Outcomes & Methods
- **All outcomes measured — Obstetric complications**: Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM); preeclampsia (overall, subdivided as preterm PE and term PE); placental abruption; placenta previa; placenta accreta; fetal distress. **Perinatal outcomes**: Emergency cesarean section; elective cesarean section; induction; postpartum hemorrhage; preterm birth (<37 weeks); stillbirth (fetal death ≥28 weeks or birthweight ≥1000 g per WHO third-trimester definition; analysis restricted to ≥28 weeks, n=108,745); Apgar score <7 at 5 minutes; neonatal asphyxia; neonatal sex; congenital malformation (including aneuploidy); low birth weight (<2500 g); neonatal death.
- **Effect measure**: RR (risk ratio) — both univariable RR and adjusted RR (aRR) from log binomial regression (with Poisson regression with robust error variance used in instances where models did not converge)
- **Statistical method**: Log binomial regression (primary); Poisson regression with robust error variance (when log binomial did not converge); chi-squared or Fisher's exact test for group comparisons; interaction term in log binomial model to test effect modification by parity (P-interaction reported)
- **Confounders adjusted**: Multivariable log binomial regression adjusted for: maternal age, prepregnancy BMI, marital status, insurance, parity, residence, education level, assisted reproductive technologies, diabetes, chronic hypertension, hypothyroidism, and antiphospholipid antibody syndrome (APS) — all variables showing significant difference in Table 1. Placenta previa and placenta accreta additionally adjusted for CS history. Low birth weight additionally adjusted for preterm birth. Missing values for BMI and education (9.7% combined) assigned to largest category in primary analysis; complete case analysis performed as sensitivity analysis.

## Group F: Quality Notes
- **Data source quality**: Large tertiary urban maternity hospital with ~16,000 annual deliveries; comprehensive electronic antenatal and delivery records with physician-coded ICD-10 diagnoses; neonatal records available until hospital discharge. Strongest dataset in terms of comprehensive confounder capture (demographics, medical history, education, ART, insurance). All women who booked but delivered elsewhere excluded. Limitation: tertiary center setting influences outcome prevalence. The single-center and urban Shanghai setting may limit generalizability.
- **Confounder adjustment**: Comprehensive — multivariable log binomial regression adjusting for 12 variables: maternal age, prepregnancy BMI, marital status, insurance, parity, residence, education, ART, diabetes, chronic hypertension, hypothyroidism, and APS. Sensitivity analyses: (1) unexplained RM vs. no RM; (2) RM vs. combined no-/single-miscarriage reference; (3) complete case analysis for BMI/education. All showed consistent results. Parity effect modification formally tested and reported.
- **Outcome validation**: ICD-10 coded by physicians; electronic records system. Preeclampsia subdivided into preterm (placental disorder) vs. term (maternal disorder) — a methodological strength. Stillbirth restricted to ≥28 weeks per WHO third-trimester definition. Missing BMI/education (9.7%) handled by assigning to largest category (main analysis) and complete case analysis (sensitivity).
- **Bias acknowledged by authors**: Yes — (1) tertiary center setting influences prevalence of some outcomes; (2) only pregnancies progressing beyond 24 weeks included (cannot generalize to all pregnancies); (3) first- vs. second-trimester miscarriages not distinguished; (4) exposure (prior miscarriages) based on self-report recorded by doctors — possible recall bias; (5) APS and some conditions documented by physician/patient report rather than strict clinical/laboratory criteria; (6) more detailed documentation of potential confounders may exist among RM patients (information bias).
- **Other notes**: This is the largest study with the most comprehensive confounder adjustment in this collection (n=108,792 total; n=1,994 RM). Key strengths: (1) preeclampsia subdivided into preterm vs. term (only preterm PE significantly associated with RM, aRR 1.58); (2) placenta accreta strongly associated (aRR 4.19); (3) parity as effect modifier formally tested — multiparous RM had stronger associations for preeclampsia, emergency CS, postpartum hemorrhage; nulliparous RM had stronger association for placenta previa, elective CS; (4) unexplained RM sensitivity analysis confirms findings for placental outcomes. The paper does not separately report a placental abruption result in Table 2 despite listing it in Methods — this may indicate non-significant or insufficient cases. GDM aRR of 1.16 (1.06–1.26) — as reported in text; note: abstract also states "aRR = 1.16; 95% CI, 1.06–1.16" which appears to have a typographic error in the abstract (upper CI should be 1.26 based on Table 2); transcribed from Table 2 as 1.06–1.26.
