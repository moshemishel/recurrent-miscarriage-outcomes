Quality Notes
Data source qualityVery large population-based single-center database (the only hospital in the Negev; no selection bias). Long study period (1988–2002). Quality controls (skilled secretaries, coding from medical records). Thrombophilia data only for 2000–2002 subperiod.
Confounder adjustmentPartial. Crude ORs for most outcomes (Tables 2, 3). Table 4 multivariate model identifies correlates of RM status — not adjusted outcome regression. For CS (Table 7): Mantel–Haenszel and multivariate analysis. No comprehensive multivariable adjustment for all outcomes. Age, parity, fertility treatments, previous CS, and comorbidities are confounders not formally adjusted for most outcomes.
Outcome validationPerinatal database with ICD-9 code 286.3 for thrombophilia; other outcomes from clinical records entered after delivery by obstetricians.
Bias acknowledgedYes — (1) higher RM incidence (4.9%) than literature (0.5–3%) attributed to ≥2 consecutive loss definition; (2) possible caregiver bias toward CS in high-risk patients; (3) most patients did not undergo thrombophilia screening (study started 1988); (4) small thrombophilia case numbers preclude firm conclusions.
Other notesThe paper's Table 4 multivariate model identifies which conditions are independently associated with being in the RM group — it is not a standard outcome regression. Key findings: placenta previa (crude OR 2.2, adjusted 1.7), abruptio (crude OR 1.6, adjusted 1.5), hypertensive disorders (adjusted 1.2), diabetes (adjusted 1.7), cervical incompetence (adjusted 12.0), CS (adjusted 1.2). Suspected IUGR, mild preeclampsia, Apgar scores, and perinatal mortality were NOT significantly associated.
