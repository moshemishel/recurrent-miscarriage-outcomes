Biological Links & Pathogenesis

This document summarizes the biological mechanisms and hypotheses proposed in the literature linking a history of recurrent miscarriage (RM) to adverse placental and perinatal outcomes in subsequent pregnancies. It is divided into two parts: exact quotations extracted from each paper, and a thematic synthesis of mechanisms by complication type.

4. Eyal Sheiner
"The etiologies of recurrent abortions are heterogeneous, and a definite cause is found in less than half of the cases [1–3]. Possible causes reported include genetic anomalies, anatomical disorders, hormonal abnormalities and thrombophilia [1–5]. Recently, an association was documented between thrombophilia and pregnancy complications such as preeclampsia [8,9], placental abruption [10], intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR) [11] and recurrent abortions [3,12–17]."
"Thus, the significant higher rates of pregnancy complications following recurrent spontaneous abortions can only speculatively be attributed to thrombophilia. 6. Jing Yang
"In previous studies, RSA has been reported to have a similar pathogenesis to that of placental dysfunction complications, such as preeclampsia, intrauterine fetal death, oligohydramnios, small for gestational age children, placental abruption, and spontaneous preterm birth. Although the late-term clinical manifestations differ, these types of diseases may have a common foundation [15]. Some scholars have named them as placental ischemic diseases [16]. Pregnancy outcome varies according to the extent of spiral artery remodeling. Partial remodeling disorder is prone to be complicated by preterm birth and FGR without pregnancy-induced hypertension; complete remodeling disorder is often accompanied with PE [17]."

8. Johanna Gunnarsdottir
"Failure of implantation has been suggested to be involved not only in the pathogenesis of miscarriage but also in pregnancy complications associated with placental dysfunction (ie, preeclampsia, stillbirth, intrauterine growth restriction, placental abruption, and spontaneous preterm birth).1-3 Implantation and placentation can be presented as a continuous process regulated by complex signaling between decidua, immune cells, and fetal tissue.3"
"Vascular adaptation of the uterus, including angiogenesis and spiral artery remodeling, is a key feature in early placental development.4 Former studies have shown that both miscarriage and placental dysfunction disorders are associated with an imbalance in angiogenic activity, disturbances in uterine blood supply, and placental oxidative stress.4-7 It has been hypothesized that a complete implantation/placentation failure may result in a miscarriage, whereas a partial failure may result in late pregnancy complications associated with placental dysfunction.5,8"
"Based on the similarities in pathogenesis of miscarriage and placental dysfunction disorders, a history of prior miscarriages might be associated with increased risk of placental dysfunction disorders."
"The results in this study are in accordance with the hypothesis that miscarriage and placental dysfunction disorders have a partially common pathogenesis of early placentation failure.8 Vascularization of the endometrium is of main importance for successful implantation and placentation.31 Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) is an important proangiogenic factor,32 and increased expression of VEGF in first-trimester decidua has been associated with both miscarriage and placental dysfunction disorders.4,33 Increased angiogenic activity and a premature onset of the maternal circulation in early placental development could result in subsequent increase in oxidative stress."
"It has been hypothesized that if these disturbances are severe, this may lead to a complete failure of early placentation and miscarriage.6,33 However, if the failure is partial, the pregnancy might remain viable but with a continued imbalance in angiogenic activity and insufficient vascular remodeling during the remaining placentation process.4"
"This possible common pathogenesis might be explained by a genetic variance affecting the endometrial control of implantation or adaptation to pregnancy.24,34,35 Genetic studies suggest that polymorphisms in the VEGF gene are associated with both recurrent miscarriages and placental dysfunction disorders.36,37"
"Placental dysfunction disorders have a recurrence risk and may predispose to each other (eg, SGA in 1 pregnancy predisposes for preeclampsia in subsequent pregnancy and vice versa).38,39 This suggests that a failure of implantation/placentation could result in placental dysfunction disorders with different clinical features in successive pregnancies. This might be explained by an interaction between mothers susceptibility to placentation failure and fetal genes,40,41 which are different in subsequent pregnancies."
"We speculate that the fetal genotype might also determine the viability of a pregnancy in a mother prone to placentation failure. If the fetus has a gene variation, making it susceptible for implantation failure, this may result in a complete failure of early placentation and a subsequent miscarriage. However, if the gene combination of the fetus is more favorable, the pregnancy stays viable but with risk of later development of placental dysfunction disorder."
"In women developing placental dysfunction disorders, the soluble VEGF receptor 1 (sFlt1) has been shown to be increased in the maternal circulation before the onset of symptoms.41,42... Recently increased sFlt1 levels in serum as well as enhanced sFLt1 expression in chorionic villus were observed in women who suffered from recurrent miscarriages.45... Furthermore, women with prior recurrent miscarriages might be prone to high expression of placental sFlt1 during a viable pregnancy but with placentation failure."

Part 2: Mechanisms by Complication Type

General / Shared Placental Complications (Preeclampsia, Placental Abruption, FGR/IUGR, Preterm Birth, Stillbirth)

  • Impaired Implantation & Placentation ("The Continuum" Hypothesis): Complete failure of early implantation/placentation results in miscarriage. If the failure is only partial, the pregnancy remains viable but suffers from insufficient vascular remodeling and placental dysfunction later in pregnancy. (Rasmark Roepke, Hautamäki, Zhang, Gunnarsdottir)
  • Vascular / Spiral Artery Remodeling Deficits: Insufficient invasion of extravillous trophoblastic cells leads to inadequate remodeling of uterine spiral arteries and placental ischemia. Partial remodeling disorder is linked to preterm birth and FGR, while complete remodeling disorder is often accompanied by preeclampsia. (Rasmark Roepke, Hautamäki, Yang, Zhang, Gunnarsdottir)
  • Immunological Imbalance: Abnormal immune cell function at the maternal-fetal interface (e.g., Th1/Th2 and Th17/Treg imbalances) impairs maternal-fetal tolerance and interferes with placentation and vascular modulation. (Rasmark Roepke, Hautamäki, Yang, Thom)
  • Angiogenic Imbalance & Oxidative Stress: Increased expression of proangiogenic factors (e.g., VEGF) and high levels of soluble VEGF receptor 1 (sFlt1) lead to premature onset of maternal circulation, resulting in early oxidative stress. (Gunnarsdottir, Zhang)
  • Trophoblastic & Decidual Abnormalities: Defective signaling between decidua, immune cells, and fetal tissue leading to fundamentally abnormal placentation. (Fawzy, Yang)
  • Thrombophilia / Hypercoagulability: Inherited or acquired thrombophilic states (e.g., Factor V Leiden) act as a shared risk factor causing early placental thrombosis (miscarriages) and late placental ischemia (preeclampsia, abruption, FGR). (Sheiner, Yang)
  • Genetic Factors & Gene-Environment Interaction: Maternal genetic variance affecting endometrial control (e.g., VEGF gene polymorphisms), and specific interactions between the mother's susceptibility and the unique fetal genotype in each pregnancy, determining whether the pregnancy miscarries or progresses to a complication. (Gunnarsdottir)

Preeclampsia / Gestational Hypertension

  • Severe Spiral Artery Deficits: Early-onset preeclampsia is directly linked to early placental implantation defects and profound spiral artery remodeling disorders (placental ischemia). (Yang)
  • Angiogenic Receptors: Overexpression of the anti-angiogenic receptor sFlt1 in the chorionic villus and maternal circulation. (Gunnarsdottir)
  • Thrombophilia: (Sheiner)

Placental Abruption

  • Secondary to Preeclampsia: A basic placental disorder causes preeclampsia, which is a major known risk factor for abruption. (Ticconi)
  • Thrombophilia / Tissue Factor Expression: (Sheiner)

Placenta Previa & Placenta Accreta Spectrum

  • Uterine Injury from Curettage: Repeated surgical interventions (D&C) for previous miscarriages cause injury to the uterine wall and endometrium, resulting in suboptimal decidualization and abnormal, deeper, or lower implantation in subsequent pregnancies. (Thom, Yang, Zhang, Murphy & Field, Sugiura-Ogasawara)

Preterm Birth & Premature Rupture of Membranes (PROM)

  • Partial Vascular Ischemia: Suboptimal spiral artery remodeling. (Yang)
  • Infection & Microbiome Dysbiosis: Imbalances in the endometrial or vaginal microbiota and reproductive tract infections can trigger both early losses and later preterm labor/PROM by altering immunological tolerance. (Hautamäki, Thom, Ali)
  • Uterine Anomalies: Structural malformations that mechanically predispose to both miscarriage and preterm birth. (Ali)

Fetal Growth Restriction (FGR / IUGR / SGA)

  • Placental Ischemia: Reduced density of trophoblastic vessels mimicking the early-onset pathogenesis of first-trimester miscarriages. (Yang)
  • Hormonal / Immunologic Defects: (Thom)
  • Maternal Genetic Trait: The existence of an inherent "birth-weight-reducing trait" found exclusively in women experiencing unexplained recurrent miscarriages. (Jivraj)
  • Thrombophilia: (Sheiner)

Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM)

  • Cumulative Diabetogenic Exposure: Women with secondary RPL have had more exposure to the natural diabetogenic effect of the second half of pregnancy from their prior live births, increasing their subsequent susceptibility. (Ticconi)
  • Shared Endocrine Disorders (PCOS): A shared etiology underlying both RM and metabolic disorders. (Jivraj, Ali)
  • Observational Bias: Higher detection due to tighter surveillance of high-risk pregnancies. (Jivraj)

Increased Blood Loss, Manual Placenta Removal & Cesarean Sections

  • Prior Uterine Procedures: Scars from previous miscarriages cause tighter adherence of the placenta, requiring manual removal. (Hautamäki)
  • Uterine Atony & Cervical Trauma: Weakness from multiple prior pregnancies/losses. (Ali)
  • Caregiver Bias ("Precious Pregnancy"): Cesarean deliveries are often chosen preventatively for high-risk RM patients rather than strictly due to immediate biological necessity. (Ali)

Malpresentation (Breech)

  • Uterine Anomalies: Underlying morphologic abnormalities of the uterus restrict fetal movement and predispose to both abortion and breech presentation. (Thom)
